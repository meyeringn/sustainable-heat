## The Problem in Plain Language

Every summer, people die from heat in American cities.
The official count is always too low — not because
officials are hiding anything, but because heat kills
quietly. It kills an elderly woman whose air conditioner
broke two weeks ago. It kills a Disabled man whose body
can't regulate temperature normally. It kills people
who were already sick, in neighborhoods that were
already struggling.

The federal government provides two tools to help cities
prepare. One tells you a dangerous heat event is coming
(NOAA HeatRisk). The other tells you which neighborhoods
are generally vulnerable (CDC Heat and Health Index).

Neither tool tells you what a city actually needs to
know five days before a heat event:

> *Which specific neighborhoods will end up in the
> emergency room this time?*

Without that answer, cities deploy resources based on
general vulnerability maps — which haven't changed
since last year, and won't change next year either.
The result is that the same neighborhoods get the
same resources regardless of whether this particular
heat event hits them hardest.

SustAInable answers the question those tools can't.

## What SustAInable Does

SustAInable is a prediction tool — not a replacement
for existing tools, but a layer on top of them.

When NOAA forecasts a dangerous heat event 48–72 hours
out, SustAInable produces a ranked list of ZIP codes
ordered by their probability of seeing elevated
emergency room visits during that specific event.

A city with 200 ZIP codes gets a list. The top 20
are HIGH risk. The next 60 are ELEVATED. The rest
are BASELINE. Cooling buses, wellness check teams,
water distribution, and emergency staffing go to
the top of the list first.

That's it. One list. Updated for every event.

## What a Pilot Would Look Like

A pilot with SustAInable requires three things:

**1. A data sharing agreement**
SustAInable needs access to emergency department
visit records during heat events — specifically,
the number of visits coded as heat illness per
ZIP code. This data exists at every major health
system and public health department. It does not
require sharing individual patient records.

**2. A point of contact**
One person at the OEM or public health department
who is willing to compare SustAInable's predictions
against what actually happened after each heat event.
Not to act on them — just to compare.

**3. One heat season**
A shadow deployment: SustAInable generates predictions
for every heat event during one summer. No operational
decisions are made based on them. At the end of the
season, we compare predictions to outcomes and
publish the results openly.

If the model performs, we talk about operational use.
If it doesn't, we publish what we learned and fix it.

## Why This Matters for Your City

Every preventable heat death costs a city more than
a prevention would have. A single heat illness
hospitalization runs $15,000–$30,000. A heat
fatality generates legal exposure, political scrutiny,
and community harm that cannot be priced.

The communities most at risk — Disabled residents,
elderly residents on fixed incomes, low-income
renters in pre-1980 housing — are also the
communities least likely to self-report, least
likely to reach out for help, and most likely to
appear in ED data *after* the window for prevention
has closed.

SustAInable is designed so that those communities
are the model's primary target — not an afterthought.
