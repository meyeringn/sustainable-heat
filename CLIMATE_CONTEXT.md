# Climate Context — SustAInable

SustAInable is not a public health tool that happens
to involve weather data. It is a climate adaptation
tool that operates at the intersection of extreme heat
events, urban infrastructure, and health equity.

---

## The Climate Trajectory

The IPCC Sixth Assessment Report (AR6, 2021) projects
that extreme heat events currently occurring once per
decade will occur every 2–5 years by 2050 under a
moderate emissions scenario (SSP2-4.5) — and every
5–10 years even under aggressive mitigation.

Urban areas face compounding risk. The urban heat
island effect — driven by impervious surfaces, reduced
vegetation, and waste heat from buildings and
transportation — adds 2–5°C to baseline temperatures
in dense neighborhoods. This amplification is not
evenly distributed: low-income neighborhoods, which
have less tree canopy, older housing stock, and lower
AC penetration, experience systematically higher
land surface temperatures than wealthier neighborhoods
in the same city.

The result is a heat mortality gradient that tracks
almost perfectly with structural inequality.

---

## Where SustAInable Fits

Two federal tools exist to help cities prepare for
extreme heat. NOAA HeatRisk answers *when* — it
provides a 5-day forecast of heat danger at the
regional level. The CDC Heat and Health Index answers
*who* — it maps structural vulnerability at the
neighborhood level based on chronic conditions.

Neither tool answers the question that matters most
for resource deployment:

> *Given this specific forecast and these specific
> neighborhoods, which ZIP codes will see elevated
> emergency room visits this time?*

SustAInable is designed to answer that question —
by combining real-time meteorological forecasts with
neighborhood-level vulnerability features to produce
a dynamic, event-specific risk score per ZIP code,
48–72 hours before an extreme heat event peaks.

This is the gap between climate *monitoring* and
climate *adaptation*. Monitoring tells you the threat
is coming. Adaptation requires knowing where to act.

---

## Why Equity Is the Design Constraint, Not the Afterthought

The communities most exposed to extreme heat are the
same communities least likely to have the resources
to self-protect — air conditioning, flexible work
schedules, access to cooling centers, medical care
for heat illness.

Disabled people face a 5x relative risk of heat
illness. Elderly residents on fixed incomes cannot
afford to run AC continuously. Renters in pre-1980
housing stock have no insulation and no legal
recourse to demand upgrades.

A climate adaptation tool that doesn't center these
communities isn't a climate adaptation tool. It's
a resource efficiency tool for cities that already
have resources.

SustAInable is designed so that the communities most
likely to be missed by conventional monitoring are
the model's primary target — not a secondary
consideration.

---

## Alignment with Climate Adaptation Frameworks

SustAInable aligns with the following frameworks
and federal priorities:

- **FEMA National Risk Index** — extreme heat is
identified as a primary natural hazard requiring
community-level risk scoring
- **CDC Climate and Health Program** — Heat and Health
Index is an explicitly cited data source and
baseline model
- **White House Environmental Justice Scorecard** —
ZIP-level heat vulnerability intersects directly
with EJ community designations
- **HUD Affirmatively Furthering Fair Housing** —
heat equity is increasingly recognized as a
fair housing issue linked to historical
redlining and disinvestment patterns
